Main Types of Context in Multimodal Misinformation

1. Textual Context

Additional text associated with the content.

Examples:

captions
hashtags
comments
article headline
linked article
OCR text in image
subtitles/transcripts
Example

Image: flood photo
Caption:

“London today after the storm.”

Context may reveal:

image actually from 2018
different country

2. Temporal Context (Time)

When the content was created or shared.

Examples:

upload date
original publication time
event timeline
repost timing
Example

Old protest image reshared during a new election.

The image itself may be real,
but the timing creates misleading interpretation.

3. Spatial Context (Location)

Where the content originated.

Examples:

GPS/location metadata
mentioned place names
landmarks
regional indicators
Example

A wildfire image claimed to be from California,
but actually from Australia.

4. Source Context

Who created or shared the content.

Examples:

account credibility
media outlet
verified/unverified source
political affiliation
bot-like behaviour
Example

Same image shared by:

official news agency
vs.
anonymous conspiracy account

The interpretation changes.

5. Social Context

How people interact with the content.

Examples:

comments
likes/shares
repost network
user reactions
community discussions
Example

Comments may say:

“This image is AI-generated.”

or

“This happened years ago.”

Social responses themselves become contextual evidence.

6. Cross-modal Context

Relationship between modalities.

Very important in multimodal misinformation.

Examples:

text-image consistency
image-video consistency
audio-text alignment
Example

Text:

“Peaceful protest”

Image:
shows violent conflict.

The misleadingness comes from cross-modal inconsistency.

7. External Knowledge Context

Information retrieved from outside sources.

Examples:

fact-check articles
Wikipedia
news reports
official statistics
search engine evidence

This is highly related to RAG-based misinformation detection.

8. Cultural / Pragmatic Context

Understanding meaning beyond literal content.

Examples:

sarcasm
irony
political symbols
memes
cultural references
Example

A meme may appear factual without understanding satire culture.

9. Reader-response Context

Very aligned with your reader-centric framework.

Examples:

emotional reactions
perceived misleadingness
audience interpretation
engagement patterns

Different readers interpret the same content differently.
